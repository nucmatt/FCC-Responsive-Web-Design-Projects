Benjamin Franklin

Founding Father and champion of America's middle class values
A portrait of Benjamin Franklin by Joseph Wright, 1782

Important Events in Franklin's Life

1706
Born in Boston on January 17th.
1718
Apprenticed to James Franklin, his brother, as a print press operator. Thus begins Franklin's life-long love of the printing press.
1722
Writes several essays under the pseudonym "Silence Dogood". Franklin would continue the publication of pseudonymous essays to advance his personal, professional, and political goals for the rest of his life.
1723
Runs away to Philadelphia after a falling out with his brother, James. He would call Philadelphia his home for the rest of his life.
1724
Sails for London on an ill-fated quest to buy equipment to set up his own print shop back in Philadelphia. His unreliable patron, Governor of Pennsylvania Sir William Keith, never supplies the letters of credit promised. Franklin would make the most of this situation and spend the next two years in London enjoying the intellectual renaissance occurring there and working at Samual Palmer's famous London print shop.
1726
Returns to America. Franklin works for Thomas Denham, a prominent Quaker merchant he befriended, as a clerk in Denham's new general store. Denham would pass shortly after returning to America and Franklin would return to the print business.
1728
Buys his own print shop with Hugh Meredith. Meredith would quickly relapse into alcoholism and Franklin would buy out his stake. From now on Franklin would always indentify himself as a printer.
1729
Buys the Pennsylvania Gazette. Franklin's original plan was to open his own newspaper to compete with the sole newspaper in Philadelphia. His plans would be leaked to the third printer in Philadelphia, who would quickly start his own newspaper first. Franklin would make use of pseudonymous essays, the 'Busy Body' essays, to criticize and poke fun at this new paper. When the owner could not turns a profit, Franklin would swoop in and buy it.
1730
Enters into common law marriage with Deborah Read. Son William is born.
1731
Incorporates the Library Company of Philadelphia, the first subscription library in America.
1732
Launches Poor Richard's Almanack. Almanacks were popular at the time and often outsold even the Bible. The Almanacks helped make Franklin wealthy. Many of Franklin's famous maxims were written in the margins of the yearly printings of Poor Richard's Almanack.
1736
Made clerk of the Pennsylvania Assembly, officially launching Franklin's political career. He also forms the Union Fire Company, modeled on Boston's fire companies.
1737
Made Postmaster of Philadelphia. It is Franklin's postmastership that drives his printing success, allowing him an early retirement.
1743
Daughter Sally is born. Launches the American Philisophical Society. Using his newspaper and postal contacts throughout the colonies, this society was made up of thinkers and scientists that shared their ideas by post and met regularly to discuss them. The American Philisophical Society thrives to this day.
1748
Retires from the printing business. This allows Franklin to devote the rest of his life to his many inventions, experiments, and politics.
1749
Proposes, raises funds for, and opens The Academy and College of Philadelphia. Franklin would remain a trustee for the rest of his life. The Academy would be merged with the University of the State of Pennsylvania in 1791 and became known as the University of Pennsylvania.
1751
Elected to the Pennsylvania Assembly. This sets Franklin on a collision course with the Penn family, the British Proprietors of Pennsylvania. The Penn family antagonism will eventually convince him that full independence from Britain is the only course for America.
1752
The famous kite and lightning electricity experiment. This leads to the invention of the lightning rod, the primary source of Franklin's fame across America and Europe at the time. He becomes especially famous in France.
1753
Becomes joint postmaster for America. It is his postmastership of all the colonies that cements the idea of the colonies as a cohesive whole in Franklin's mind.
1754
Drafts the Albany Plan of Union, an alliance between the colonies for their common defense during the French and Indian War. While rejected by all the colonies, Franklin's Albany plan contained the seeds of federalism, the basis for a unified American nation.
1757
Sent to London as a diplomatic agent for Pennsylvania. During this time his feud with the Penn family escalates towards outright hostility. Franklin's years in London society win him many friends, allies, and admirers.
1762
Returns to Philadelphia, his diplomacy a mixed bag. Franklin's son William, is soon appointed Governor of New Jersey with lobbying help from his father. This will place father and son on opposite sides of the coming conflict.
1764
Defeated in a bitter Pennsylvania Assembly election as ugly as any seen in modern politics. Franklin's faction does win a majority however and votes to continue Pennsylvania's fight against the Penn family. Franklin is again sent to London as the Assembly's agent.
1766
Testifies against the Stamp Act in British Parliment. The Stamp Act is promptly repealed, only to be replaced the following year by import duties known as the Townshend duties, that included import taxes on tea.
1770
Made the London agent for Massachussets. Franklin wins the repeal of the Townshend duties, except on tea. It is Franklin's work representing Massachussets that leads to his most explosive confrontation with British Parliment.
1774
Removed as Postmaster after his role in sending purloined private letters from Thomas Hutchinson, governor of Massachussets, to the Massachussets Assembly. Prior to his removal, Franklin endures scathing criticism and personal attacks during the supposed hearing on the Massacussets petition to remove Thomas Hutchinson as governor. Held in a Parlimentary room known as the Cockpit, where cock fights were held during Henry VIII's reign, this explosive confrontation cements Franklin's American patriotism while winning him near heroic status amongst the colonies and with colonial sympathizers throughout England. Deborah Franklin passes.
1775
After returning to Philadelphia from London, the battles of Lexington and Condord occur. Franklin is elected to the Second Continental Congress and proposes the Articles of Confederation and Perpetual Union. These Articles contain the framework for the later Articles of Confederation.
1776
The Declaration of Independence is signed. Though written by Thomas Jefferson, Franklin had the honor of performing the final edits to Jefferson's masterpiece. After signing the Declaration, Franklin is sent on a diplomatic mission to France. He will spend the entirety of the Revolutionary War negotiating for America's interests from Paris. William Franklin is removed as Governor of New Jersey and imprisoned.
1778
Treaties of alliance and commerce with France signed. Franklin and John Adams butt heads as both are diplomats to France. Franklin also meets, and develops an admiration for, John Paul Jones. Franklin goes so far as to acquire Jones an old man-of-war, the Duras, for Jones' naval adventures. The Duras is rechristened the Bonhomme Richard and goes on to defeat the HMS Serapis.
1782
Negotiates, alongside John Adams and John Jay, the peace treaty with Britain after the Cornwallis surrender at Yorktown. Franklin would stay in France for several more years to negotiate treaties, loans, and alliances with multiple European powers.
1785
Returns to Philadelphia as an American hero. He would spend the next couple of years in leisure, pursuing his own whims and interests. History, however, intervenes and Franklin's wisdom, wit, and instinct for compromise will soon be needed again.
1787
The Constitutional Convention of 1787 is held in Philadelphia. Franklin is elected as one of Pennsylvania's delegates. Franklin would nominate George Washington as chairman of the convention, serve as a symbolic host to many delegates, and wrote the most eloquent words of his life in giving the closing address of the convention.
1790
Passes on April 17, at the age of 84. The opening words to his last will and testament: I, Benjamin Frankin of Philadelphia, printer.

"Benjamin Franklin is the Founding Father that winks at us. He seems made of flesh rather than marble, addressable by nickname, and he turns to us from history's stage with eyes that twinkle from behind those newfangled spectacles.

--Walter Isaacson in Benjamin Franklin, An American Life
